Capture Mode

Enabling professional interior designers to capture the space they design from a more effective perspective so that they are better able to show off their designs to their client.


Winter 2019 & Spring 2021

UX Designer: research, ideation, interaction and interface design, user flows, prototyping, usability testing, UI documentation

The Problem

Interior designers using the room planner application are only able to create and present room plan layouts to their customers from an overhead view. For some people who aren’t as tall as Shaq, this isn’t the view that they would normally see their room in. This unnatural perspective makes it difficult for some customers to really visualize their space.

The Solution

A way for people to see their room from a more natural perspective— from an eye level view.

Set Up Camera Angle

Use the camera cone to define exactly how you want the shot to be taken. The camera can be dragged around anywhere and can pivot to any angle.

Take Photos in the Photo Capture Modal

Adjust the view of your shot by being able to step back or forward to capture more or less of the image. Save the image to share with clients.

View and Share your Photos

View your saved images in the Photo Board and create collages to share with clients.

The Process


Market Research

In order to create a meaningful tool for our designers, we explored other relevant online experiences that allow users to view their surroundings in a variety of perspectives; there was no exclusive focus on room planning tools specifically. We wanted to understand how other companies utilized different views and what made them successful or unsuccessful.

Ideation

Based off of our constraints, and how we understood the tech that was developed, we decided to explore the idea of image capturing. We initially moved towards the direction of having a live view feature that would aid users as they designed, but because lack of resources caused us to be unable to render images fast enough, this concept was not doable for our timeline and we had to quickly pivot.

We held rapid brainstorming sessions utilizing the crazy 8’s method to ideate on different key screens.

Testing

We tested on a variety of people— our coworkers in-house to get some quick feedback on early wireframes and designers who would be using this feature to get their feedback on the more fleshed out design work. Our methods included sitting down with people and seeing how they accomplish tasks, as well as sending out surveys to understand how this feature could be more useful to their needs.

Feedback: People Want Flexibility

One of the biggest pieces of feedback we got was that users wanted more flexibility in capturing their photo. We initially had gone in the direction of having users select furniture items to set their focal point, but that was found to be too rigid and users weren't able to capture the angle they wanted. We pivoted to a more flexible angle selection method that allows users to drag the camera anywhere and angle it at any degree.

Feedback: Remove Image Cap Limitations

As we increased our resources and changed the way images are stored in the backend, this removed the need to set limitations on how many photos each user could take. We removed the photo count and are reserving that space for future feature capability.

Next Steps

  • Improve the image quality.
    While the images here provide a better sense of reality, we have a ways to go when it comes to life-like imagery.
  • Bring warmth and personality to a space.
    At the moment, the focus has been on furniture in a space, but that's not the only thing that ties a space together. Adding capability to include items such as decor and wall pieces can better sell a space.

Takeaways

  • Inspiration can be found anywhere, even in very different products.
    We found inspiration in products that were outside of the home space and found that it provided a unique perspective to tackling parallel problems.
  • Be flexible and ready for any sudden changes.
    A solution that we thought would work best for designers and their clients ended up not being possible to come to fruition because of lack of resources, so we had to quickly move towards the next best thing.
