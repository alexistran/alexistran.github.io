Williams-Sonoma

Help Center

Enabling designers and everyday consumers to be efficient and self-sufficent to find answers to any questions they have about Room Planner on their own.


Spring 2019

UX Designer: research, feature prioritization, user flows, ideation, interaction and interface design, UI documentation

The Problem

There are two versions of Room Planner— one for your everyday at-home consumer, and an enterprise version for Williams Sonoma's professional interior designers. If a user needs help with anything related to Room Planner, they have to email customer support. This is neither the quickest nor most efficient way to get help, especially if they just have a simple question.

There was no way for users to get the help that they need on their own.

The Solution

Helping users get the help that they need quicker and gain a better understanding of all that Room Planner has to offer.

Making a tedious task more enjoyable

Sometimes finding help on your own is overwhelming. I kept the home screen of the help center very simple, only allowing users to choose between 3 options: FAQ's, Updates, and a Contact form if they need more help.

I created illustrations for each category to not only illustrate what each category is, but to bring a little more joy onto the page.

There are also several access points for the user to get back to Room Planner after getting the help that they need.

Retaining familiarity

I retained the same menu bar style that exists in Room Planner so that it's very clear to the user the connection between the application and the help center. The menu bar allows access to all the content in the help center from any page.

Easily find answers

There are FAQ's, Updates, and Search. If the user still can't find what they're looking for from the content available, there is an easy-to-access contact button on every page.

The last resort

If a user can't get the answers that they need on the help center, there is still hope.

The contact modal pops up and grays out everything around it so that the user can focus on the task at hand.

The Process


Identifying Problems through Market Research

I started off by exploring the experience of getting help from other companies. I looked at and used the help center of 14 different companies to understand how other companies help you find the answers to questions you have.

I created a feature inventory chart to help me keep track of all these features, especially those that made my experience notable.




After I gathered this information, I analyzed it in a features prioritization chart to help me figure out which key features are necessary for our MVP.

Site Map

I created a general site map to capture the key pages our site will contain.

User Flow

I constructed a user flow to understand the paths that our two main types of users (consumer vs enterprise) could take through the help center to get the help that they would need. This helped me understand all the potential ways users could interact with this product and catch any edge cases.

Ideation

I began by sketching out different ideas and creating wireframes of the most viable concepts. I sought feedback from my fellow designers and tested paper prototypes on people around the office. After many rounds of design iterations, I arrived to a more final design.

Takeaways

  • Be okay with pivoting.
    Moving deep down one direction and ultimately needing to pivot back to a previous concept can be frustrating, but it's just part of the process.
  • Hierarchy is important in making dense information more consumable.
    This can be achieved in a variety of ways, like playing with text, color, spacing, etc.
